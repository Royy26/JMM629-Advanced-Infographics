THE QUESTION

What can you know before episode one?

Industry insiders often read a season before it starts: they watch the market, the studio slate, the poster, and the names attached to a project. We asked whether a model could detect those same clues using only information available before broadcast.

We collected data on 1,403 TV anime released during 2000–2025 across five clues available before broadcast:

📊
M — Metadata
Release year, genre, time slot, and other scheduling context. In plain terms: what kind of market the show is entering.
🎨
I — Image
Poster features. Captures the first visual impression a viewer or editor gets.
📖
T — Text
The synopsis. Captures how the story is described before anyone watches it.
🎙️
C — Cast
Voice actor information. A clue about familiar names attached to the show.
🏭
P — Production
Studio and production credits. A clue about the industry backing behind the project.

We are not asking which anime is "best." We are asking which early clues most reliably point to a future TV hit. This model does one simple thing: before a show airs, it ranks new titles by how closely their pre-release clues resemble anime that later reached the TV ratings chart.

A growing industry

From 2000 to 2015, Japan's TV anime output nearly doubled. A show never enters a neutral market; it arrives in a season with a certain scale and level of competition.

02

The expansion years

By the mid-2000s, more titles were fighting for attention. Release year, timing, and season became clues in their own right.

03

Peak output, same pressure

Even when total output peaks, the share that reaches weekly ratings stays fairly steady. That makes metadata especially useful: the model is reading where a show enters the field.

04

Why context stays powerful

This is why metadata remains the strongest single clue. The model is not just reading a title; it is reading metadata before viewers react.

TAKEAWAY

What did the model learn?

"Before episode one airs, the model reads a show's metadata more clearly than the plot summary inside it."
01
Metadata is the strongest clue. On its own it reaches 0.6122, well above the other single-clue versions. Year, time slot, and broadcaster all help the model read a show's starting position before viewers react.
02
Image helps more than Text. Image-only beats Text-only, and the strongest pair is Metadata + Image at 0.6456. Image carries useful information that Text does not always capture.
03
Time slot gives a strong, actionable baseline. After splitting by slot, the same model family remains informative in every group, and the top APs become easier to interpret by context: Morning 0.8081, Afternoon-Eve 0.7585, Late Night 0.2921.
04
Station analysis turns performance into strategy. The station profile explains why results differ: NTV starts with a much higher chart rate (47.4%) than TV Tokyo (8.3%) or TBS (7.5%). This helps identify where Image or Text adds the most practical value.
